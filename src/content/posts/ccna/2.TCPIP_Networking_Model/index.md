# History Leading to TCP/IP

**OSI model** was developed in the late 1970s and early 1980s by the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) as a framework for understanding and designing network protocols. It was intended to provide a common language and structure for network communication.

However, the **OSI model** was not widely adopted in practice, and instead, the TCP/IP protocol suite became the dominant standard for network communication. TCP/IP was developed in the 1970s by the United States Department of Defense as a way to enable communication between different types of computer networks. TCP/IP was designed to be flexible and scalable, allowing it to support a wide range of applications and network architectures.

**TCP/IP** was adopted by the Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF) as the standard protocol suite for the Internet, and it has since become the foundation for modern network communication. The success of TCP/IP can be attributed to its simplicity, reliability, and ability to adapt to changing network requirements.

Overall, the history leading to TCP/IP highlights the importance of practical implementation and adaptability in the development of network protocols. While the **OSI model** provided a theoretical framework for understanding network communication, it was the practical implementation of TCP/IP that ultimately led to its widespread adoption and success in the networking industry.
